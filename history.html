The Chronicles of the Nobel Prize

An exhaustive look at the legacy, the will, and the evolution of the world's most prestigious award.

Alfred Nobel: The Merchant of Death?

Born in Stockholm in 1833, Alfred Nobel was a man of paradoxes. A pacifist who invented dynamite, and a recluse who became a global industrialist.

The turning point for the prize occurred in 1888. When Alfred’s brother Ludvig died, a French newspaper mistakenly published Alfred’s obituary instead. It was titled "The Merchant of Death is Dead," condemning him for profiting from the invention of explosives. This deeply affected Nobel, who decided to change his legacy to ensure he would be remembered for contributing to peace and knowledge.

"It is my express wish that in awarding the prizes no consideration be given to the nationality of the candidates, but that the most worthy shall receive the prize, whether he be Scandinavian or not."

Alfred Nobel, The Will of 1895

A Century of Milestones

Year Historical Event Significance
Nov 27, 1895 The Last Will is Signed Alfred signs his will at the Swedish-Norwegian Club in Paris, allocating 94% of his fortune (31 million SEK) to the prizes.
Dec 10, 1896 Death of Alfred Nobel Nobel dies of a cerebral hemorrhage. His will is read, shocking his family and the Swedish public.
1901 The First Ceremony The first prizes are awarded. The physics prize went to Wilhelm Röntgen for X-rays; the peace prize to Henry Dunant (Red Cross).
1914-1918 World War I Many prizes were not awarded during the war years, reflecting the global instability and the Nobel Committee's difficulty in gathering information.
1944 The "Einstein" Era During WWII, the prize for Physics (1943) was awarded to Otto Stern. The prize played a role in highlighting the moral weight of nuclear physics.
1968 Economics Added Sveriges Riksbank donated funds to create the Prize in Economic Sciences, bringing the total number of categories to six.

Notable Historical Records

The Only Two-Time Winner in Different Sciences

Marie Curie remains the only person to win Nobel Prizes in two different scientific fields: Physics (1903) and Chemistry (1911).

The Oldest vs. Youngest

John B. Goodenough won at age 97 (Chemistry), while Malala Yousafzai won the Peace Prize at just 17.

Famous Refusals

Jean-Paul Sartre (Literature 1964) and Le Duc Tho (Peace 1973) are the only two people to have voluntarily declined the prize.

The Nobel Banquet

Every December 10th, a lavish banquet is held at the Stockholm City Hall, where the menu is kept a secret until the very last moment.
